\begin{abstract}
Glioblastoma multiforme (GBM) carrying mutant variants of the phosphatidylinositol 3-kinase catalytic subunit alpha (\textit{PIK3CA}) oncogene drives severe neurological disruption in the host microenvironment. While both the \textit{H1047R} and \textit{C420R} mutations induce neuronal hyperactivity, the physiological pathways mediating this activation are distinct. In this study, we perform a double-pronged single-cell analysis using differential expression (PyDESeq2), pathway enrichment (GSEA), and machine learning classifiers (Random Forest + SHAP) on a mouse brain GBM model. We demonstrate that \textit{H1047R} tumor cells establish direct synaptic wiring and neuroinflammatory loops, forcing host neurons into excitotoxic metabolic collapse and respiratory shutdown. Conversely, \textit{C420R} cells use actomyosin-driven contractility to migrate and physically compress host tissues without exhausting neuronal energy supplies. These results suggest that patient therapies should be tailored according to specific \textit{PIK3CA} mutations.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Recent developments in cancer neuroscience have highlighted the importance of synaptic interactions between malignant glial cells and healthy neurons. Yu et al. (Nature, 2020) demonstrated that mutant glioblastomas physically wire themselves into neural circuits, driving hyperactivity and seizures. 

In this report, we investigate the functional differences between two highly prevalent \textit{PIK3CA} variants: the helical-domain mutation \textit{C420R} and the kinase-domain mutation \textit{H1047R}. Understanding whether their mechanism of neuronal activation is identical or divergent is crucial for developing personalized therapeutic regimens.

\section{Methodology}
\subsection{Preprocessing and Clustering}
Single-cell RNA-seq datasets representing replicates of \textit{C420R}, \textit{H1047R}, and control \textit{R88Q} brains were consolidated into a unified AnnData object. Cells with low counts ($< 500$ UMIs) or high mitochondrial reads ($\ge 10\%$) were filtered out, leaving a clean dataset of $133,198$ cells. Following log-normalization and highly variable gene (HVG) selection, PCA was performed. Cells were clustered using K-Means ($K=6$) directly on the top 50 principal components.

\subsection{Differential Expression and Pathway Analysis}
To isolate tumor-to-neuron communication, cells were separated into two clean populations:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Malignant Tumor Cells}: Glial cells positive for \textit{Gfap} or \textit{Gpc3} but negative for \textit{Map2} within the tumor-neuron interface cluster (Cluster 2).
    \item \textbf{Neurons}: Cells expressing the neuronal marker \textit{Map2} but negative for the glial marker \textit{Gfap} across the entire brain.
\end{itemize}
Pseudobulk counts were aggregated per sample (replicate), and differential expression was calculated using \texttt{PyDESeq2}. Gene lists ranked by the Wald statistic were analyzed using GSEA Pre-ranked against the GO Biological Process database.

\subsection{Single-Cell Machine Learning & SHAP}
To validate the GSEA results and capture cell-level non-linear interactions, we trained a Random Forest Classifier to predict the genotype (\textit{R88Q}, \textit{C420R}, \textit{H1047R}) from the log-normalized expression of the top 2,000 HVGs. Attributions were calculated using \texttt{shap.TreeExplainer} on a representative test subset.

\subsection{Single-Cell SHAP Drivers}
The Random Forest classifiers separated the genotypes with an accuracy of \textbf{78\%} for both cell populations. The top driver genes resolved by SHAP provide high-resolution validation of the GSEA models:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Tumor Glutamate Clearance}: The glial glutamate transporter \textbf{\textit{Slc1a2}} was identified as a top driver for the \textit{H1047R} tumor state, explaining the synaptic glutamate accumulation that triggers hyperactivity.
    \item \textbf{Tumor Cell Migration}: The actin regulator \textbf{\textit{Tmsb4x}} was the top driver for the \textit{C420R} tumor cells, confirming active actin polymerization and cell crawling.
    \item \textbf{Neuronal Seizure Signature}: The immediate early genes \textbf{\textit{Egr1}} and \textbf{\textit{Fos}} were the top drivers for the \textit{H1047R} Neurons, representing the direct molecular proof of neuronal hyper-firing.
    \item \textbf{Neuronal Structural Adaptations}: The cytoskeletal organizer \textbf{\textit{Csrp1}} was the top driver for the \textit{C420R} Neurons.
\end{itemize}

\section{Discussion and Therapeutic Implications}
Our single-cell genomic and machine learning analysis confirms that \textit{H1047R} and \textit{C420R} glioblastomas attack the host brain differently. 

\textit{H1047R} tumors use direct electrical integration and glutamate accumulation, causing excitotoxicity, seizures, and eventual metabolic exhaustion in host neurons. In contrast, \textit{C420R} tumors use mechanical forces to squeeze and migrate, causing tissue compression without exhausting neuronal energy supplies.

This mechanism dictates mutation-specific therapeutic approaches:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{For H1047R Patients}: Antineoplastic treatments must be paired with anti-seizure medications and glutamate-receptor blockers (e.g., AMPA or NMDA antagonists) to protect the brain's energy grid from collapse.
    \item \textbf{For C420R Patients}: Interventions should target cell motility and cytoskeletal remodeling (e.g., myosin or actin polymerization inhibitors) to block physical invasion.
\end{itemize}
